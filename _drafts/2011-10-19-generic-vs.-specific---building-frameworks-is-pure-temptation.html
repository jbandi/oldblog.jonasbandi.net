Responsibility trap number one: Building a platform to make other (lesser) programmers more productive.

- Eric Evans at SET 2008

Everybody working on large scale frameworks today should have a look at the entertaining presentation by Stefan Tilkov: Generic vs. Specific.



 

My favorite provoking statement from the presentation:

Some programmers, when faces with a problem turn to a generic solution ... now they have two problems.



I have seen so many unsuccessful or semi-successful attempts at creating "The Framework" that I get immediately very suspicious when I come to a project that is using a (homegrown) framework.

Of course in todays enterprise IT it is difficult to tell the difference between a library, a technology stack/platform and a framework. However

Usually the intent behind building "The Framework" was to build something more specific as the existing technology stack/platform or the common libraries. But what they usually end up with is a more generic solution than when implementing the required system on the given platform.
The promise is to be more productive with this custom generic foundation than with only the generic platform. However this is difficult to measure and often I have the impression that compared to the specific solution the semi generic solution takes 50% to build the framework, 20% to implement the usecases that fit to the indeded usages of "The Tramework" and another 50% to implement that unforseen usecases that do not fit "The Framework".

 

Another funny observation from the presentation is the phases in a developers life:

  1. The Enthusiastic Developer - "Lets build programs for real people"
  2. The Disillusioned Developer - "Real people have boring problems"
  3. The Enthusiastic Architect - "Lets build the generic thing machine"
  4. The Disillusioned Architect - "YAGNI, KISS"
